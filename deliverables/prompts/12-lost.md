# The Museum of Lost Things — Hall of Ephemera

## Full Reproduction Prompt

Build a single-page dark-academia digital museum called "The Museum of Lost Things — Hall of Ephemera": a candlelit catalogue of four obsolete objects — the card catalog, the public telephone box, the compact cassette, the paper road map — floating over a real-time WebGL hall of dust motes, drifting lost artifacts, and cold spotlights rendered with three.js.

Palette: near-black ink #23201B page background (deeper variant #1B1815), warm parchment #E9E2D0 text, oxblood accents #5C1F1F (bright variant #7A2C2C), antique brass #9C7C46 for rubrics and hairline rules. Typography: Playfair Display (medium italic) for display headings and drop caps, EB Garamond for body prose, IBM Plex Mono for catalogue numbers, rubrics, dates, and footnote markers.

Centerpiece: behind the whole page, on a fixed full-viewport canvas (three.js r147 from a CDN), render a fog-drowned museum interior — a dark floor, a colonnade of pillars marching into the murk, and roughly 2,400 dust motes drawn as a single additive shader point cloud. Each grain drifts on seeded sine paths, fades with camera distance, and scatters softly away from the cursor by pushing vertices in NDC space. Suspend six low-poly lost artifacts built purely from primitives — a brass key, a folded paper plane, a pocket watch, an oxblood button, an umbrella, a folded letter with a wax seal — at increasing depths; they rotate imperceptibly slowly and bob as if underwater, fade out just before the camera passes through them, and hovering near one (raycast against an invisible halo sphere) lifts it toward the light with an emissive glint. Light the hall with two spotlights on a rig that follows the camera — one cold blue-grey, one candle-warm — each with a visible additive gradient cone shaft. Scrolling glides the camera about seventy units deeper past the artifacts; mouse position adds gentle camera parallax so nearer layers shift more. Cap devicePixelRatio at 2, cut the mote count on screens ≤768px, pause the render loop when the tab hides or the canvas leaves the viewport, remove the canvas entirely if WebGL or the CDN fails so the static design stands alone, and under prefers-reduced-motion render single still frames re-composed discretely on scroll.

Signature 2D technique: each exhibit is a light parchment "museum plate" card carrying a hand-drawn engraving built entirely from inline SVG strokes. Give every path a pathLength of 1 with the dash fully offset, then transition the offset to zero when an IntersectionObserver marks the plate as seen, staggering stroke groups with per-element delay variables so each illustration draws itself line by line. Layer two fixed radial-gradient candle glows that flicker on a slow opacity-and-scale keyframe loop over the 3D hall, plus a fixed vignette and repeating-gradient page-edge textures. Plates sit in a two-column grid with even cards offset downward; hovering lifts a plate and unfolds a hidden monospace acquisition note via a grid-rows zero-to-one transition.

Sections in order: entrance-hall hero (mono kicker, italic title, fleuron, descend thread), Permanent Collection of four plates with footnote superscripts, Curator's Note blockquote, Marginalia footnotes with target highlighting and return links, Visitor Book footer on ruled-paper background with colophon.

Copy: elegiac, literary museum-label prose. Three.js is the only dependency; everything else is hand-written in one HTML file, and the page must degrade gracefully to a settled, static museum when WebGL is unavailable.
