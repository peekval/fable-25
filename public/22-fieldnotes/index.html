— Portugal

everything I owned

Thirty-one days, one pen that leaked over Spain, and a paper habit I couldn’t shake. What follows are the pages that survived — drawn standing up on trams, on harbour walls, on café tables I held far longer than one bica justifies.

page one · Lisboa

The 28 climbs Graça

You don’t ride the 28 so much as get adopted by it. It grinds up out of Baixa with its whole wooden body complaining, takes the corner at Sé close enough to the walls that you could pass a postcard through a window, and everyone aboard leans as one congregation.

I drew this standing up, elbow hooked round a brass pole, and the line wobbles exactly where the track does. I’ve decided that’s not an error — that’s documentation.

page two · Porto

Blue arithmetic

Porto keeps its accounting on its walls. Every façade is a ledger of squares — glazed, hand-painted, faded unevenly so the pattern shimmers like water when you walk past. I stood across from a house on Rua das Flores and tried to copy one panel honestly.

Somewhere around the ninth tile the pattern stopped repeating and started breathing. The tilers left small deliberate errors, an old habit. I left several accidental ones, a new habit.

page three · the coast

Where the land runs out

Past Cascais the road narrows, the pines lean inland like commuters, and then the continent simply stops. The lighthouse stands there doing the one job Europe still needs done by hand at this address: telling the Atlantic where the furniture is.

I anchored the sketchbook with a rock and my left knee. Every page after this one carries a faint salt crust, which I recommend as a fixative — cheap, atmospheric, slightly corrosive, like the coast itself.

page four · still life

A table held all afternoon

The waiter and I reached an understanding by the second day: I would order a bica every ninety minutes, and he would pretend the table was not needed. The espresso arrives already half-drunk, it’s that small. The pastel de nata arrives already half-gone, that’s on me.

A grilled sardine on brown paper completed the composition. It watched me draw it with the flat scepticism of a fish that has seen many tourists and outlasted none of them.

fim

closing page

Sent home, eventually

real stamps still exist.
this thrilled me

I posted the loose pages to myself from a yellow box near the harbour, because a sketchbook that travels home separately gets one more story than you do. They arrived two weeks after I did, smelling faintly of somewhere better.

If these notes make you buy a pen before a plane ticket, they’ve done their job in the right order.

— drawn en route, mended at home
