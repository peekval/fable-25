How it was built

The hero city does not exist in any file: fourteen buildings are defined purely as data — grid position, footprint, height, name, programme — and projected at runtime with true 30° axonometric math into SVG polygons, painter-sorted by depth, dropping in with a springy stagger and sprouting leader-line spec labels on hover. The centerpiece, though, is a real 3D axonometric tower: a three.js scene on a fixed full-viewport canvas behind the sheet, shot through an OrthographicCamera locked to the classic 30°/45° drawing angle. Every volume is a flat-shaded box with three fixed face tones (paper top, two facade greys) plus an EdgesGeometry wireframe in drafting blue — drawn, not lit. Scrolling the project index assembles it storey by storey, scrubbed to scroll: slabs glide in from offset positions, columns rise from their slabs, facade panels snap onto the two visible faces, while a coral service core climbs as the grounded spine. Hovering a project row lifts and coral-tints its storey band; pointer-x rotates the whole model a few degrees with inertia. After the pinned plan→volume SVG morph (a 1:100 plan lerped via matrix() into isometry and extruded), a second pinned sheet pulls all the floors apart into an exploded axonometric along a dashed datum axis and reassembles them, and by the contact block the finished building settles into the title-block corner, gently orbiting. No WebGL falls back to the flat drawing; reduced motion shows the completed tower as a static frame; the DPR is capped at 2 and the render loop pauses offscreen.
