01 Inside the vessel

Camera four looks at a working star through ten centimetres of fused quartz.

During a shot, the inboard camera records what no person will ever stand near: deuterium–tritium plasma circulating the torus at hundreds of kilometres per second, glowing where it brushes the edge of the field. The wall it never touches stays at room temperature.

Fig. 01 · visible-light emission at the plasma edge. The core, at 150 million °C, is too hot to glow in visible light at all.

02 The machine

T-1 is small enough to build in four years, and strong enough to hold ignition.

High-temperature superconducting magnets changed the arithmetic of fusion. Field strength enters plasma performance to the fourth power — so doubling the field shrinks the machine by an order of magnitude. T-1 is that bet, in 1,100 tonnes of steel.

03 Roadmap

Sober dates. One immodest goal.

Every milestone below is gated by the one before it. We publish shot data quarterly, including the failures — fusion has been twenty years away for seventy years, and the only cure for that is arithmetic in public.

2027

First plasma

T-1 vessel sealed, coils at field, first deuterium shots. Milliseconds of confinement, terabytes of calibration.
