Act I — The Season

Three plays, one long night

No. I · Main Stage

The Cartographer of Smoke

Sept 18 — Nov 2 · Thu–Sun

A mapmaker is hired to chart a city that burned down forty years ago, using only the arguments of its survivors. Each act redraws the map; each redraw costs a witness their favorite lie. By the finale the stage holds a city no one lived in and everyone misses — and the cartographer must decide which grief gets a street named after it.

by Ilse Marchetti · dir. T. Abara

No. II · The Pit

Winter Choir

Nov 20 — Jan 11 · Wed–Sat

Nine strangers shelter in a mountain radio station as the avalanche warning hardens into fact. The transmitter still works; the road does not. What begins as a broadcast of reassurances becomes a confession sung in shifts — a choir assembled by catastrophe, rehearsing the one song you only learn when someone might be listening for your voice under the snow.

by Corin Vale · dir. M. Okonjo

No. III · Main Stage

The Glass Prompter

Feb 6 — Mar 29 · Thu–Sun

An actress at the end of a legendary career begins hearing her lines one breath before she thinks them — whispered from the prompter's box, which the theatre sealed in 1963. Is it memory, machinery, or the role finally learning her? A chamber piece about authorship of the self, performed inside a set that slowly turns to face the audience it accuses.

by A. den Hartog · dir. T. Abara

Act II — The House

A mill that learned to whisper

VELUM occupies the carcass of a nineteenth-century velvet mill, and it has never forgiven us for it. The looms are gone but their rhythm stayed in the floorboards; on quiet nights the ushers swear the building keeps time. We kept the iron columns, the burn marks, the freight door tall enough to admit a cathedral prop or a very proud director.

The auditorium seats three hundred and eleven — never three hundred and twelve, a superstition we inherited with the deed and honor without explanation. There is no proscenium arch. There is only the curtain: eleven meters of crimson cloth, re-woven every decade by the last workshop in the region that still dyes with madder root, hung so close to the first row that the front seats come with a gentle warning about drafts.

We believe a theatre should be slightly haunted and entirely honest. One of these we can guarantee.

Act III — Director's Note

On the subject of cloth

People ask why we spend a restoration budget on a curtain when the roof, frankly, negotiates with the rain. Here is the answer I give the board, and it is also true: the curtain is the only honest actor we employ. It does not interpret. It hangs, it breathes with the draft from the freight door, and when it opens it makes the single clearest promise in all of art — something is about to be shown to you on purpose.

Everything we program lives inside that promise. The plays this season are about maps made of testimony, songs made of fear, and a voice arriving one breath early. Different stories; one obsession: the moment before revealing, held as long as the cloth can bear it.

Come early. Watch the curtain move before anything is behind it. That ripple is the whole of theatre; the rest of us are commentary.

Tomas Abara

Artistic Director, VELUM
