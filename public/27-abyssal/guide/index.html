ABYSSAL · Build Log · Site 27

How the descent was built

One page, one dive: from sunlit water to an anglerfish at 4,000 metres — no libraries, one canvas, and two AI-generated specimen plates logged mid-descent.

The whole page is a single continuous descent: scroll position maps linearly to depth, a fixed monospace meter counts from 0 to 4,000 m, and the background is interpolated live between colour stops for the sunlight, twilight, midnight and abyss zones, so the water darkens as you fall. Everything animated lives on one full-viewport canvas behind glass content panels: marine snow whose density changes by zone and whose parallax streaks upward as you scroll, godray beams that dissolve below roughly 400 m, and a school of about sixty boids-lite fish that scatter away from the cursor. The jellyfish are drawn entirely in code — a bell built from sine-pulsed bézier curves with tentacle chains that trail behind using simple follow-through physics, so they lag and whip like fabric in water. All glows are pre-rendered once onto small offscreen canvases and stamped with drawImage, instead of per-frame shadowBlur, which keeps hundreds of particles at 60fps; pixel ratio is capped at 2, the loop pauses when the tab is hidden, and reduced-motion visitors get static painted zones. At the bottom, an anglerfish silhouette is drawn after the light pass so its black body occludes its own lure — a cursor-following spotlight that is the only light left in the abyss.

Generated Assets

The two ROV observation plates are the only photographic material on the page, and both are AI-generated. The specimen stills — a bioluminescent crown jelly and a chain siphonophore glowing like a constellation in black water — were made with Nano Banana 2 on Higgsfield (text-to-image, 2K, 4:5 portrait), prompted as deep-sea ROV photography: lights off, black water, marine snow, nothing but the animal's own light. The jellyfish plate was then brought to life with Kling 3.0 Turbo (image-to-video): a slow bell pulse and drifting snow, about 4.5 seconds, which was made to self-loop by crossfading its tail into its head with ffmpeg and encoded as h264 at CRF 26 (344 KB, silent); the stills were optimised to WebP the same way. On the page they sit in thin cyan/violet hairline frames with Space Mono specimen IDs whose depth labels were calibrated against the live HUD, an inset porthole vignette dissolves their black grounds into the dive water, and the loop plays only while on screen — an IntersectionObserver pauses it offscreen and reduced-motion visitors get the poster still instead.

Reproduction Prompt

Build a one-page website for "ABYSSAL", a fictional deep-sea research institute's public outreach site. The entire page is one continuous dive: scrolling descends from the surface to 4,000 m, with a fixed monospace depth meter counting down the metres and naming the zone (sunlight, twilight, midnight, abyss). Interpolate the background colour between zone stops as depth increases. Palette: abyss black-blue #030812, bioluminescent cyan #3DF5FF, violet glow #8A5CFF, pale specimen white #DCE9F0. Fonts: Spectral (light serif, scientific-poetic headlines) and Space Mono (depth readouts, labels).

A full-viewport canvas behind the content renders: drifting marine-snow particles whose density varies by zone and which streak upward while scrolling; godray light beams that fade out below ~400 m; a school of ~60 small boids fish that flee the cursor; two or three procedural jellyfish with sine-pulsing bézier bells and trailing tentacles using follow-through physics, glowing softly; and, in the deepest zone, an anglerfish silhouette whose glowing lure follows the cursor as the only light — a radial-gradient spotlight the black body occludes.

Content floats as frosted-glass panels at specific depths: surface hero, mission (~470 m), twilight-zone specimen cards (~1,000 m), midnight jellyfish gallery (~1,700 m), abyss expedition call-to-action (~3,400 m), seafloor footer. Wonder-filled but scientifically accurate copy. Mood: dark, quiet, luminous, vast. Keep it at 60fps: pre-rendered glow sprites, particle caps, capped pixel ratio, single animation loop, reduced-motion fallback.

Add two ROV observation plates mid-dive, framed like scientific specimen photographs shot through a porthole. Generate the specimens with Nano Banana 2 (Higgsfield, text-to-image, 2K, 4:5): (1) "bioluminescent crown jellyfish photographed by a deep-sea ROV, lights off, cyan and violet glow, black water, drifting marine snow, only the animal's own light"; (2) "giant chain siphonophore glowing violet and cyan like a living constellation in black water, deep-sea ROV photography". Animate the jellyfish still with Kling 3.0 Turbo (image-to-video): slow bell pulse, gently drifting marine snow, camera static, ~5 s. Post-process with ffmpeg: stills to WebP; video to silent 720×720 h264 CRF 26, self-looping via a crossfade of the clip's tail into its head, plus a matching WebP poster frame. Mount each plate in a 1px cyan (jelly) or violet (siphonophore) hairline frame on a #010309 ground with an inset vignette so the black photo dissolves into the water, a "REC · HALCYON-9 · CAM 2" header, and a Space Mono caption with fictional specimen ID and a depth that matches the live meter (~2,300 m for the jelly video, ~3,100 m for the siphonophore).
