How the page reaches 10 mK

44-kelvin is one HTML file, no libraries. Its conceit is that the page itself is a dilution refrigerator: scrolling is descent, and the environment obeys the thermometer.

Temperature-driven environment

Each stage section carries a data-temp attribute (300, 50, 4, 0.8, 0.01). On scroll, the viewport midpoint is located between the midpoints of the two nearest stages and the temperature is interpolated logarithmically — the only honest way to travel four and a half orders of magnitude. A fixed IBM Plex Mono readout formats the result (K above one kelvin, mK below) and names the current stage.

From the log temperature a 0–1 --cool factor drives everything else: the body background lerps from warm lab white toward blue-white, a frost-noise texture (an inline SVG feTurbulence data-URI, radial-masked so it only touches the viewport edges) fades in as cool², and — the systems joke played straight — --dur stretches every transition duration from 0.35 s at room temperature to ~1.4 s at base. The colder the page, the slower and more precise its movements.

The Bloch sphere

A DPR-aware canvas draws a wireframe sphere: a gold equator, two latitude rings, one meridian, a dashed z-axis with |0⟩ and |1⟩ pole labels, all projected through a fixed camera tilt. Pointer position sets target θ and φ; the state chases them with an exponential smoothing that reads as cryogenic patience, and precesses slowly when idle. The vector is the site's single iridescent element — a violet→cyan gradient stroke with a glowing tip. Measure samples the Born rule (P(|0⟩) = cos²(θ/2)), then snaps the vector to the winning pole with an easeOutBack overshoot and a fading gold ring. The rAF loop pauses whenever the canvas leaves the viewport or the tab hides; with reduced motion there is no loop at all — steering applies instantly and measurement collapses without animation.
