FACET · build notes

Cutting a diamond out of mathematics.

The stone

The hero is raw WebGL — no library. The round brilliant is generated procedurally from rotational symmetry: an octagonal table ring, a 16-point girdle ring, a half-step-rotated pavilion break ring and a culet point, stitched into 112 triangles (table 8, crown 24, girdle 32, pavilion 48). Every triangle duplicates its vertices and carries a per-face normal, so each facet shades as one flat, decisive plane.

There is no environment map. A tiny shader function invents the studio: a near-black velvet base with three razor-sharp vertical softbox strips (cosine powers of 90–260) plus one broad dim fill. Because a facet's refracted direction is constant across its surface, each facet samples exactly one point of that environment — so facets snap between black, platinum and blazing white as the stone turns, which is what a real brilliant does.

Dispersion is faked twice. The refraction runs per channel with three different etas, and each channel also rotates the environment a few degrees — thin rainbow fringes appear where a facet straddles a light edge. On top of that, "fire": each facet hashes its normal into a hue, and that spectral flash is gated by the local luminance gradient of the environment, so it only ignites where light is actually dispersing. A faked pavilion bounce (reflect the refracted ray, flip it upward, resample) lifts light back through the table.

The pointer, heavily damped, rotates the environment and tilts the stone — the fire migrates across the crown as you move. DPR is capped at 1.5; if the frame rate sits under 14fps the loop stops and the last frame stands as a still-life render. Reduced motion draws exactly one considered frame; no WebGL falls back to a CSS conic-gradient gem; the loop pauses offscreen and when the tab hides.

Caustics on velvet

Below the stone, a 2D canvas paints the light the gem throws: ellipse-squashed arc segments and rare spectral threads inside a soft radial pool, phase-locked to the same rotation value the shader uses. The layer's edges are erased with destination-out so the canvas has no visible boundary against the velvet.

The atelier scrub

"The cut" is a 520vh track with a sticky stage, scrubbed by scroll position. An irregular rough-stone polygon gives way to five cleave lines drawn on with stroke-dashoffset; then a face-up cut plan — generated in JS from the same rotational symmetry as the mesh — reveals its 57 elements one at a time (table, 8 stars, 8 kites, 16 upper-girdle, then the pavilion's dashed mains and lower-girdle lines) while a mono counter keeps score. A gradient polish sweep, clipped to the girdle circle, passes once; five thin spectral arcs settle on the finished plan. Stage captions I–V change with the phase.

Generated asset

The vitrine photograph — a 2-carat round brilliant held in tweezers over black velvet, rainbow caustics scattered beneath — was generated with Nano Banana 2 via the Higgsfield MCP and finished with ffmpeg into a 1400×1738 WebP (assets/diamond.webp, ~170KB). On the page it loads lazily with real dimensions and reveals like a vitrine: brightness raised from 0.12 to 1 under a descending radial spotlight when scrolled into view, with an engraved small-caps caption.
