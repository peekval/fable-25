The Ledger of Years

Heritage, 1928 to the present day

Every grand hotel keeps two registers. One records the guests. The other — kept rather more carefully — records the years. Ours begins on a spring evening between the wars, and it has never once been closed.

  1. 1928

    Opening Night

    On the evening of May the fourth, two thousand guests in white tie ascended beneath a canopy of searchlights. The Meridian opened with forty-one storeys, six hundred rooms, a ballroom floored in Cuban mahogany, and the stated intention of making every other hotel on the avenue obsolete by morning.

  2. 1931

    The Midnight Broadcast

    From a velvet-draped studio on the mezzanine, the Meridian Orchestra played live across the continent every Saturday at midnight. The bandleader, it is recorded, refused to raise his baton until the elevator men confirmed that the penthouse was dancing.

  3. 1937

    The Empress Arrives

    An empress in exile took the entire thirtieth floor for a season. She left behind her name, a standing order for jasmine tea at four o'clock, and the corner suite that still carries her monogram in gold leaf above the door.

  4. 1942

    The Quiet Years

    The ballroom traded its orchestra for long tables and tin cups, serving as a canteen for officers on embarkation leave. The chandeliers were crated, catalogued, and hidden in the sub-cellar — every crystal accounted for upon their return in 1946.

  5. 1968

    The Long Dusk

    Fashion moved elsewhere, as fashion does. The gilding dulled; the orchestra dwindled to a trio, then a pianist, then a phonograph. But the doors never closed, and the night porter kept the register in fountain pen until his final shift in 1981.

  6. 2024

    The Restoration

    Five years. Eleven thousand hours of gilding. One stubborn conviction: that nothing should look new, and everything should work perfectly. Six hundred rooms became one hundred and eighty. The phonograph was retired with honours. The orchestra returned.

The bar at the top of the Meridian

Beneath a ceiling of hammered gold leaf, forty-one floors above the traffic, the longest marble bar in the city pours the same house cocktail it poured on opening night. Jackets after seven. The elevator man knows the way.
