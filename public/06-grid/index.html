Müller-Brockmann called the grid “an aid, not a guarantee.” Issue №07 tests that claim across six lives spent inside columns: from the Tonhalle posters of Zürich to the tiled mosaics of the New York subway.

Six essays. Twelve columns. One argument — that the strictest systems produce the freest work.

01

The Zurich Axiom

Josef Müller-Brockmann’s concert posters for the Tonhalle — beginning with the 1955 “beethoven,” its concentric arcs sized in strict geometric progression — argued that music could be notated visually. His 1981 manual “Grid Systems in Graphic Design” turned that instinct into law, still the discipline’s most-quoted rulebook.

Systems
Zürich, 1955–1981

02

Twenty-One Univers

In 1957 Adrian Frutiger released Univers with Deberny & Peignot — not a typeface but a planned family of 21 weights and widths, mapped on a numeric matrix (45, 55, 65…) instead of vague names like “bold.” It was type design as engineering, decades before variable fonts made the idea native.

Typography
Paris, 1957

03

The Athlete, Reduced

For Munich 1972, Otl Aicher drew athletes as pure geometry — limbs snapped to a grid of horizontals, verticals and 45° diagonals — and chose a palette of light blue and green that pointedly refused the colours of 1936. His pictograms became the world’s de-facto wayfinding language.

Identity
Munich, 1972
11 min read
→
04

Subway to Standard

Massimo Vignelli’s 1972 New York subway diagram allowed only 45° and 90° angles, one dot per station, and geography be damned — beloved by designers, contested by riders, retired in 1979. His Unigrid system for the U.S. National Park Service (1977) proved the same rigour could quietly run for decades.

Information
New York, 1972
10 min read
→
05

The Counterform Case

At the Basel school, Emil Ruder taught that white space is not leftover but load-bearing: the unprinted counterform gives the printed form its meaning. His 1967 “Typographie” — trilingual, exhaustively systematic — remains the quiet counterweight to Zürich’s louder dogma.

Pedagogy
Basel, 1967
8 min read
→
06

Programmes, Not Solutions

Karl Gerstner’s “Designing Programmes” (1964) proposed that a designer’s real product is not the artefact but the rule-set that generates it — morphological boxes, parameter grids, combinatorial logic. Read today, it is a manifesto for computational design written before the computer arrived on the desk.

Method
Basel, 1964
