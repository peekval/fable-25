The Museum of Lost Things

How it was built

The whole site is one hand-written HTML file — no framework, no build step, no image assets; three.js (r147, from a CDN) is the single dependency. Behind the page a fixed WebGL canvas renders the hall itself: a fog-drowned interior with a dark floor and a colonnade fading into the murk, some 2,400 dust motes drawn as one additive shader point cloud — each grain drifts on seeded sine paths, fades with depth, and scatters softly away from the cursor in screen space — and six low-poly lost artifacts (a brass key, a paper plane, a pocket watch, a button, an umbrella, a folded letter with its wax seal) built purely from primitive geometry. Two light shafts, one cold and one candle-warm, ride a rig that follows the camera; scrolling glides the camera seventy units deeper down the aisle, and hovering near an artifact lifts it toward the light with an emissive glint. DPR is capped at 2, the mote count drops on small screens, the render loop pauses when the tab hides or the canvas leaves view, reduced-motion visitors get discrete still frames, and if WebGL or the CDN is missing the canvas removes itself and the candlelit page stands alone.

Every "engraving" is an inline SVG drawn stroke by stroke: each path is normalised to a length of one and starts fully dash-offset, then an IntersectionObserver flips a class when the exhibit scrolls into view and the strokes transition to zero offset, so the illustration draws itself line by line, with per-group delay variables staggering cabinet, drawers, and details. The candlelit atmosphere layers two fixed radial-gradient glows on slow, out-of-phase flicker keyframes over the 3D scene, plus a fixed vignette and page-edge textures. Each exhibit sits on a parchment "museum plate" — layered radial gradients fake brass foxing and oxblood shadow — and hovering a plate lifts it while a hidden acquisition note unfolds through a grid-rows transition. Footnotes use native anchor targets with a highlighted ledger rule.
