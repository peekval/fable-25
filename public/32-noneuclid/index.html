01 / tHE BIENNALE

buildings that refuse to be built

NON-EUCLID exhibits architecture whose only construction material is mathematics. Each structure in this year's programme is a signed distance field — a single equation folded against itself until it produces corridors, vaults and courtyards that no crane could assemble and no surveyor could certify.

The premise of the biennale is deliberately narrow. We do not show renderings of buildings, because a rendering is a photograph of a decision already made. We show the deciding function itself, evaluated live, sixty times per second, on whatever machine you happen to be holding. The building above is being computed as you read this sentence. Move your cursor and it will notice.

Architecture has always trafficked in the nearly-possible: cantilevers at the limit of steel, domes at the limit of masonry. The three structures gathered here abandon the "nearly." They are rigorous in every respect except buildability — internally consistent, dimensionally exact, and physically absurd. We consider this an honest trade.

02 / tHE StRUCtURES

three commissions, zero kilograms

01

the recursion hall

AtELIER SIEBENFACH, BASEL

A civic assembly hall subdivided by strict trisection: each of its chambers is the hall again, at one-third scale, with its own chambers, with their own. The architects specified occupancy for the outermost shell only; every deeper floor is jurisdictionally unresolved. Visitors describe the interior as "a parliament arguing with its own echo."

02

pavilion of folded light

M. OKONKWO & DAUGHtERS

A garden pavilion whose walls were designed flat and then folded — not the drawings, the walls. Space near the pavilion is creased like paper, so its slender colonnade shelters a floor area several times larger than its footprint. The practice insists this is merely efficient planning taken to its logical conclusion.

03

the inverted basilica

StUDIO VREDE, ROttERDAM

A basilica turned outside-in: the nave wraps the sky, the façade faces inward, and the crypt is the highest point. Load paths run upward, which the structural report describes as "correct, given the premises." It is the only church in the programme, and the only structure visitors report exiting before they have entered.

03 / CURAtOR'S ESSAy — EXCERPt

against the right angle

"We ask architects to be honest about gravity and then punish them for being honest about geometry. The right angle is not a law of nature. It is a habit of the ruler."

— DR. ILSE BRANDt, CURAtOR, FROM tHE CAtALOGUE INtRODUCtION

Every building you have entered was a compromise between an idea and a material. The idea wanted to continue; the material wanted to stop. What the distance field offers — and what this biennale takes seriously — is a material that never wants to stop. Fold it and it folds exactly. Repeat it and it repeats without fatigue, without tolerance stack-up, without a single change order.

The criticism writes itself: these are not buildings, because nobody can stand in them. But nobody can stand in a plan, either, and we have granted plans the full dignity of architecture for five hundred years. The plan was always the building's most truthful form — the built object merely its expensive approximation. We have removed the approximation. What remains is disquietingly pure: circulation without floors, thresholds without doors, a section that continues past the depth where sections normally admit they were guessing.

Walk the render above slowly. Notice that the fog is doing moral work: it forgives the structure its infinite detail, the way distance forgives a city its scaffolding. That forgiveness is the oldest trick in architecture. We have simply written it down.
