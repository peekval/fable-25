How this was built. Off the record.

The method, in daylight

The flashlight now has a body: a raw-WebGL fragment shader on a fullscreen premultiplied-alpha canvas renders volumetric cigarette smoke — two layers of domain-warped fbm (value noise under a rotation matrix, amplitude-normalized so 3 octaves on phones matches 5 on desktop) that drift, curl, and brighten only where light reaches them. The pointer is the desk lamp: a warm scattering core, a working beam, and a wide halo illuminate the fog from inside, while the smoke recoils around a second, lagged copy of the light position and slowly re-forms once the beam moves on. Venetian-blind stripes rake diagonally through the haze, and every so often a passing car sweeps a band of light along them; the lamp flickers in occasional storms; an iris vignette closes the frame. Everything composites in the shader — shadow falloff, unlit wisps silhouetting inside the beam, luminous fog — and outputs premultiplied RGBA straight over the page, so the hero copy burns through legibly at the beam's core. The pointer drives it, touch drives it, and after 3.5 idle seconds the beam wanders a slow lissajous so the office never looks dead. DPR is capped at 2, the loop pauses when the tab hides or the canvas leaves view, and when WebGL is missing the original compositor-only CSS torch (one 300vmax translate3d element) takes over; reduced motion gets a still office. The typewriter copy lives in the markup for no-JS visitors — a script stashes it and retypes it with randomized delays, caret blinking in blood red. Around that: hover-to-reveal redacted spans, manila case folders hinging open through rotateX(−72°), and animated feTurbulence grain over the lot. Playfair Display speaks; Special Elite testifies.
