A museum of scale

MAGNITUDE

One continuous descent through thirty-six orders of magnitude — from the filaments of the cosmic web to a single atom. Every scene below is drawn in code. Scroll, and fall.

Begin the descent
10²⁶ m · ten billion light-years

The cosmic web

At this scale, galaxies are dust. They gather along filaments of dark matter — the largest known structures — wrapped around voids so empty that light crosses hundreds of millions of light-years without meeting anything.

10²¹ m · one galaxy

The Milky Way

Roughly 100,000 light-years across, holding between 100 and 400 billion stars. The Sun rides one of the outer arms, completing a single orbit of the galactic centre about every 230 million years.

10¹³ m · one solar system

The Sun's neighbourhood

Sunlight takes about four hours to reach Neptune — and over four years to reach the next star, Proxima Centauri. Almost everything here, by volume, is nothing at all.

10⁷ m · one planet

A living world

Earth is 12,742 kilometres wide, yet nearly all of its weather lives in a film of air about a thousandth of that. Seen from orbit, the entire atmosphere is one thin blue line.

10⁻¹ m · one leaf

The vein network

A leaf's veins branch until no green cell sits more than a few cells from water. Nearly every molecule of oxygen you have ever breathed was released by photosynthesis like this.

10⁻⁵ m · one living cell

The cell

A typical human cell measures a few hundredths of a millimetre. You are a cooperative of roughly 37 trillion of them — and every single one descends from one fertilised cell.

10⁻¹⁰ m · one atom

Carbon

An atom is about a ten-billionth of a metre wide, and almost entirely empty space. Its nucleus is tens of thousands of times smaller — yet carries more than 99.9% of the mass.

End of descent

You are here.

Between the cosmic web and the atom lie thirty-six powers of ten — and you live near the middle of them. On the logarithmic line from a single atom to the Sun, a human being stands close to the exact centre. Too large to feel the quantum world, too small to feel the curvature of space: the middle of it all is where noticing happens.
