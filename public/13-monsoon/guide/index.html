MONSOON

an atmospheric journal — three weathers, recorded in ink & water

How it was built

MONSOON is a single HTML file that paints its entire visual world at runtime — no photographs, no video, no image assets of any kind. A fixed background layer sits behind the page and continuously blends its color between per-section sky stops, keyed to the scroll position of the viewport's center with smoothstep easing, so the journal drifts from storm slate through rain blue, fog grey and golden clearing to near-black nightfall.

A small frosted SVG barometer floats in the corner the whole way down; its gold needle eases between angles assigned to each section and its readout word flips from STORM to FAIR as you pass each weather. Each full-height chapter owns its own canvas simulation, started and stopped by an IntersectionObserver: rain drawn as three depth planes each batched into a single stroked path, with splashes and ground haze; fog as pre-rendered radial-gradient sprites drifting on sine paths behind halo-protected serif text; the clearing as twinkling dust motes under blurred, screen-blended god-rays.

The rain chapter carries three live storm systems. Lightning strikes rarely — every 12 to 20 seconds a recursive branching bolt is generated from jittered segments steered back toward vertical, forking into thinner channels, drawn as a soft glow pass under a hot core; a double-pulse sky flash momentarily brightens the whole palette through a fixed wash layer, and thunder arrives a beat later as a 300ms two-pixel rumble of the section. Wind gusts every 8 to 15 seconds push the rain visibly diagonal while the chapter's heading leans letter by letter — skewed spans with staggered delays and a spring return. And the downpour itself is scrubbed by scroll velocity: fast scrolling wakes a reserve of extra drops, lengthens the streaks and whips the fall speed, while stillness lets the storm settle back to calm. Everything remains deliberately hand-drawn code — no generated assets, no photographs, no images of any kind. Under prefers-reduced-motion the storm goes quiet: one composed still frame, no lightning, no shake, and the pixel ratio is capped to keep fill cost bounded on high-density screens.
