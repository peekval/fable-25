How it works

Three small rituals. Nothing to keep up with.

Somnium is not a streak to defend or a score to chase. It asks for less than a minute at either edge of the night — and stays out of the middle entirely.

i.

Wind down

One question before bed — how heavy does today feel? — answered with a swipe. The screen dims itself as you answer, so the last thing you see is already half asleep.

ii.

Sleep

Somnium does nothing while you sleep. No microphone, no radar, no glow. Phone face-down, app closed. The night belongs to you.

iii.

Catch the dream

Morning capture is built for half-closed eyes: giant type, voice first, three-word entries welcome. Whatever you salvage, Somnium files it before it fades.

Most of a dream is gone within minutes of waking — which is exactly when every other app asks you to unlock, log in, and find the right tab.

Somnium opens straight into capture. Speak it, type it, or tap three words from your own recurring vocabulary. Entries can be fragments; fragments are the point.

Over weeks, the journal quietly maps your recurring places, people and moods — so one morning you notice the staircase has been visiting you for years, and it always shows up before a big decision.

A note on the science

Sleep is not an off switch. It's a tide.

Somnium leans on what sleep research broadly agrees about — and refuses to dress the rest up as fact.

Sleep moves in cycles

A night is a series of roughly 90-minute cycles, drifting between deeper and lighter stages. Waking from a light stage feels kinder — which is why mornings differ so much even at the same alarm time.

Dreams cluster late

REM sleep, where vivid dreaming concentrates, grows longer toward morning. The dream you can almost touch at 7 a.m. was probably minutes old — reach for it fast.

Slow exhales calm

Long, slow breaths out engage the body's rest-and-digest response. You can't force sleep, but you can make the runway to it much smoother.

Recall is a skill

People who note dreams regularly tend to remember more of them over time. The journal doesn't just store your dreams — it slowly turns the volume up.

Somnium is a journal, not a medical device. If your sleep is persistently painful, that deserves a real clinician — not another app.
