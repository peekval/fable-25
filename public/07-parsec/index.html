01 The ascent

Getting here is half the observation.

PARSEC sits on the summit ridge of Piz Larun, reached by a 14-minute funicular from Vnà station. The last car up leaves at 21:40; the first car down waits until the domes close. Dress for the altitude — we provide the blankets, the thermoses and the sky.

2,742m

Altitude

Above two-thirds of the atmosphere's water vapour. Transparency here is what lowland observers only read about.

21.9mag

Sky darkness

Zenith sky brightness of 21.9 mag/arcsec² — a certified Bortle Class 1 site, one of eleven in the Alps.

1.0m

Main reflector

A one-metre Ritchey–Chrétien under the north dome, open to the public every clear night. No qualifications required. Curiosity is enough.

300+nights

Clear skies a year

The Larun ridge sits above the valley inversion. When the villages below are under fog, we are usually above it, watching.

02 The catalogue

Hover a field. Let the lines find themselves.

Four of the fields our guides return to every season. On the ridge we draw them with a laser; here, your cursor will do.

The Swan

Cygnus

The Northern Cross, flying south along the Milky Way. Albireo, at the head, splits into gold and sapphire in the eyepiece.

Lyra

The Queen

Cassiopeia

The great W, circumpolar from the ridge — she never sets. Our pointer to a hundred open clusters along the winter Milky Way.

Orion

Deep field · drawn in code, seen in glass

The faint stuff is the point.

Anyone can find the Moon. Our guides live for the objects at the edge of vision — the Veil's filaments, the outer arms of M51, the green flash of a comet's coma. On a Class 1 night, the one-metre pulls them out of the dark like photographs developing.
